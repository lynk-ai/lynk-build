The 2026 FIFA World Cup was the first edition to expand the field to 48 teams, up from 32 — sourced (Wikipedia "2026 FIFA World Cup" and FIFA.com). The 48 teams were split into 12 groups of four (Group A through Group L); the top two finishers in each group advanced automatically, joined by the eight best third-placed teams across all 12 groups, into a newly created knockout round: the Round of 32. That round replaced the Round of 16 as the tournament's first knockout stage. The larger field and the added round pushed the total match count from 64 (in the 32-team era) to 104 — sourced, same source.

The tournament ran 39 days, opening 11 June 2026 and closing with the final on 19 July 2026 — sourced (Wikipedia). The opening match was played at Estadio Azteca ("Estadio Banorte") in Mexico City: Mexico beat South Africa 2–0 in a match that saw three red cards, leaving Mexico with 10 men and South Africa with 9 — sourced (ESPN/CNN/FIFA.com). That result began an unbeaten Mexico run that carried into the knockout rounds, covered in [upsets-and-storylines](upsets-and-storylines.md).

Hosting was split across three countries and 16 cities: 11 in the United States (Atlanta, Boston, Dallas, Houston, Kansas City, Los Angeles, Miami, New York/New Jersey, Philadelphia, the San Francisco Bay Area, and Seattle), three in Mexico (Mexico City, Monterrey, Guadalajara), and two in Canada (Toronto, Vancouver). The US venues carried the bulk of the load, hosting 78 of the 104 matches, with Mexico and Canada splitting the remaining 26 — sourced (Wikipedia / FIFA.com host-cities pages). For the security and infrastructure side of the US hosting effort specifically, see [host-preparations](host-preparations.md).

The knockout results that followed this format — who advanced through the new Round of 32 and beyond — are covered match-by-match for the finalists and semifinalists in [knockout-routes](knockout-routes.md), and the tournament's final outcome in [the-final-and-podium](the-final-and-podium.md).
