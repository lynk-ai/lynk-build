This is the "hard numbers" companion to [four-failure-modes](four-failure-modes.md), for multi-agent systems specifically — the same role [why-context-rots](why-context-rots.md) plays for long context. Before any number: **as of July 2026 there are no publicly measured per-run failure or incident rates for multi-agent LLM systems in production.** That is an absence claim, so mark it accordingly — derived from a sweep of the public record (benchmark papers, vendor telemetry, practitioner surveys, engineering blogs), and uncertain in the way absence claims always are: unpublished internal numbers may exist. Everything quantified below is benchmark-harness measurement, infrastructure telemetry, or self-report survey — and knowing which is which is most of this chapter's value.

**What is measured: benchmark failure rates.** The MAST study (Cemri, Pan, Yang et al., *Why Do Multi-Agent LLM Systems Fail?*, arXiv 2503.13657 v3, NeurIPS 2025 Datasets & Benchmarks) measured failure rates across state-of-the-art open-source multi-agent frameworks; the 6 shown here span 41% to 86.7% (Figure 5 / Appendix B) — all sourced:

| Framework | Benchmark | Failure rate |
|---|---|---|
| AppWorld | Test-C | 86.7% |
| HyperAgent | SWE-Bench Lite | 74.7% |
| ChatDev | ProgramDev | 66.7% |
| Magentic-One | GAIA | 62.0% |
| MetaGPT | ProgramDev | 60.0% |
| AG2 | OlympiadBench | 41.0% |

The paper itself cautions these run on *different benchmarks* and are "not directly comparable" — the range is honest, the ranking is not. The numbers deserve weight as measurement: the taxonomy behind them came from grounded-theory analysis of 150+ traces (6 experts, >20 hours annotation each), three annotators reached Cohen's κ = 0.88, and the scaling LLM-as-judge annotator (OpenAI o1, few-shot) hit 94% accuracy and κ = 0.77 against human experts, κ = 0.79 on two unseen systems (Sections 3.2–3.4, Table 2).

**Where the failures land.** Over MAST's full 1,642-trace dataset: system design / specification issues 44.2%, inter-agent misalignment 32.3%, task verification 23.5% (Figure 1, v3). Citation hazard, sourced to the same paper: the widely quoted 41.8% / 36.9% / 21.3% split is the earlier version / the 210-trace human-annotated subset (Figure 4 in v3) — mixing versions makes two quotes of the same paper contradict each other. The most prevalent individual modes: step repetition 15.7%, reasoning–action mismatch 13.2%, unaware of termination conditions 12.4%, disobey task specification 11.8%, incorrect verification 9.1%, no/incomplete verification 8.2%; rarest is information withholding at 0.8% (Figure 1). Note this is MAST's own taxonomy, not the context-failure taxonomy in [four-failure-modes](four-failure-modes.md) — related lenses, different studies; don't merge their categories.

**How much design fixes buy.** MAST's ablations (Sections 4–5, Appendix H / Table 5): improving agent role specification alone raised ChatDev task success +9.4% (same GPT-4o, same prompt); adding a high-level task-objective verification step raised ChatDev +15.6% on ProgramDev. The paper's stated ceiling for system-design-only interventions is that +15.6%, with its own caveat that "task completion rates still remain low" — prompts and structure help, but don't close a 60–87% failure gap. Model and architecture choice measurably shift the *profile*: within MetaGPT, GPT-4o showed 39% fewer system-design failures than Claude 3.7 Sonnet; on the same ProgramDev benchmark, MetaGPT had 60–68% fewer specification/misalignment failures than ChatDev but 1.56× more verification failures (Section 5.1, Appendix B.3/F). Note that MAST's two largest categories — specification issues and inter-agent misalignment, 76.5% combined per the v3 split above — are exactly what the brief-and-isolation discipline in [isolation-and-strict-briefs](isolation-and-strict-briefs.md) exists to prevent (derived: the categories match that chapter's failure surface; MAST did not evaluate that pattern).

**Measured comparisons cut both ways.** For multi-agent: Anthropic measured its production research system (Opus 4 lead + Sonnet 4 subagents) at 90.2% improvement over single-agent Opus 4 on an internal research eval, at ~15× the tokens of chat; on BrowseComp, token usage alone explained 80% of performance variance, 95% with tool-call count and model choice (Anthropic engineering blog, *How we built our multi-agent research system*, June 2025). Note what that post does *not* contain: any error or incident rate — it is an internal capability eval of a production system, not production reliability telemetry. Against: Smit et al. (ICML 2024, arXiv 2311.17371) found multi-agent debate strategies "do not reliably outperform" single-agent self-consistency/ensembling, and a follow-up across 5 MAD frameworks, 9 benchmarks, 4 models (arXiv 2502.08788) found MAD "often fail to outperform" single-agent CoT/self-consistency at significantly higher compute (the paper's abstract gives no per-setting count — "often" is as precise as the source gets). And a widely circulated skeptical piece, Cognition's *Don't Build Multi-Agents* (2025), contains zero measured numbers — cite it as practitioner position, never as evidence.

**What production evidence actually exists — proxies only.** All sourced:

- *Call-level telemetry, not MAS-level:* Datadog's State of AI Engineering (telemetry from thousands of customer companies) measured 5% of all LLM call spans erroring in Feb 2026 (60% of those rate limits), dropping to 2% by March 2026. It also found 59% of agentic requests make only a single service call and 18% make 3+ — "most agents are still monolithic" — so this telemetry can't even be attributed to multi-agent architectures, let alone give a per-run rate.
- *Behavioral constraints, not failure rates:* UC Berkeley's *Measuring Agents in Production* (Pan et al., arXiv 2512.04123; 306 practitioner responses, 20 case studies, 26 domains) — 68% of production agents execute at most 10 steps before human intervention, 74.2% rely on human-in-the-loop evaluation, reliability is the top-cited bottleneck. It contains no original production failure-rate measurements; its numbers describe how tightly agents are leashed, which you may read (derived) as revealed practitioner belief that unleashed failure rates are unacceptable — but that is an inference, not a measurement.
- *Self-report, no denominator:* SailPoint/Dimensional Research (May 2025 IT-professional survey): 80% of companies said their AI agents had taken unintended actions — 39% unauthorized system access, 33% sharing sensitive data. Agent-general, not multi-agent-specific, and "80% of companies had ≥1 incident" is not a failure *rate* — there is no per-run denominator.

**The laundering hazard — why you will meet "production" numbers anyway.** Circulating claims that "70–95% of AI agents fail in production" are laundered adoption statistics: they trace to MIT NANDA's 2025 finding that 95% of internal AI *pilots* fail to achieve *adoption*, and Gartner/McKinsey pilot-to-production figures (McKinsey 2025: <10% of respondents have agents beyond pilot). Worse, secondary blogs (e.g., Augment Code's "fail at rates between 41–86.7% in production") splice MAST's benchmark range onto the word "production" — a claim the primary paper never makes. This is derived: cross-checking the MAST v3 PDF (benchmark-only provenance) against the secondary posts quoting the same range as "production". The test to apply to any such number: does it have a per-run denominator, and was it measured in a harness, a survey, or live traffic?

**The strongest honest citation is: benchmark-measured failure rates of 41–86.7% (MAST, not directly comparable across frameworks), with production evidence limited to proxies — and no public per-run production failure rate exists to cite (as of July 2026; derived absence claim).** If someone hands you a cleaner number than that, its provenance is the first thing to check.
