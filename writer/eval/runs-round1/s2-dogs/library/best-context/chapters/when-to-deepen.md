This is the judgment you face when a home outgrows itself: split *sideways* (more pages at the same level) or *deepen* (add a new discovery layer — an `index.md` of its own, so a page becomes a book). Both are progressive disclosure; they differ in whether you add breadth or depth. Split sideways when the home is still one concept, just too long or buried under caveats: the short current page keeps the name and specifics move to sibling pointer pages (the page-budget discipline). Deepen a level when a sub-part has become several concepts a reader cites independently and needs its own discovery layer: the sub-part earns its own `index.md` and becomes a book, and the old home shrinks to a stub (the graduation discipline).

The tell for deepening is that you find yourself wanting an index for the sub-part. An index is a discovery layer, and you only need one when there are several things to disclose. Don't deepen a single concept (an index over one page is pure overhead), and don't flatten a cluster (a pile of sibling pages with no index is an un-navigable shelf). **Deepen a level only when a sub-part has grown its own cluster of independently-cited concepts that need their own index; short of that, split sideways.**

Progressive disclosure is a worked example of the judgment: as a single chapter it grew several independently-cited concepts and was split out into its own indexed home — then, when that cluster proved thinner than a standalone home needs, it folded back into a core chapter plus a few siblings. The judgment runs in both directions: deepen when a cluster needs its own index, fold back when it thins to what siblings can carry.

Both moves can be rule-enforced — an oversized page warns toward a sideways split, and turning a page into a book without leaving a stub behind is rejected — while this chapter is the *judgment* that decides which applies, so a writer isn't left guessing.
