Start with the negative finding, because it reframes every number below: **true production task-level failure telemetry for multi-agent LLM systems does not publicly exist.** No company has published task-level multi-agent failure percentages from live traffic (derived — the negative result of a systematic search across papers, vendor telemetry reports, and engineering blogs as of July 2026, and the reasoning is checkable below: the three closest candidates each disqualify — Anthropic's number is an internal eval, Datadog's numbers are span-level infrastructure errors, and the one preprint claiming production data is unverifiable). Every failure rate you can actually cite is a *benchmark* rate — measured on curated task suites, not live traffic. The distinction matters because the two are routinely conflated, and the conflation is where the fake statistics come from.

The best-measured benchmark numbers come from MAST ("Why Do Multi-Agent LLM Systems Fail?", Cemri, Pan, Yang et al., UC Berkeley, arXiv:2503.13657, ICML 2025). Across 7 state-of-the-art open-source multi-agent frameworks, measured failure rates ranged 41%–86.7%; the table below shows the 6 systems with named per-system rates in the findings this chapter draws on — the 41%–86.7% range spans all 7 measured systems (sourced: v3 PDF, Figure 5 and Appendix B):

| System | Benchmark (model) | Failure rate |
|---|---|---|
| AG2-MathChat | OlympiadBench / GSM-Plus (GPT-4) | 41.0% |
| MetaGPT | ProgramDev (GPT-4o) | 60.0% |
| Magentic-One | GAIA (GPT-4o) | 62.0% |
| ChatDev | ProgramDev (GPT-4o) | 66.7% |
| HyperAgent | SWE-bench Lite (Claude-3.7-Sonnet) | 74.7% |
| AppWorld | Test-C (GPT-4o) | 86.7% |

The paper itself warns that each system was measured on a different benchmark, so these rates are not directly comparable across systems (sourced: same paper) — read the table as "each framework fails a large fraction of its own benchmark", not as a ranking.

MAST is also the first empirically grounded taxonomy (the paper's own claim) of *why* these systems fail: 14 failure modes in 3 categories, with measured frequencies from 1,600+ annotated traces in the current v3 — Specification issues 37.17%, Inter-agent misalignment 31.41%, Task verification 31.41%; the top single modes are "disobey task specification" at 15.2% and "step repetition" at 11.5% (sourced: arXiv:2503.13657 v3, Figure 2). A citation trap, itself verified by comparing the two revisions: the earlier v2 (a 150-trace analysis) reported different numbers — 41.77% / 36.94% / 21.30% by category, step repetition 17.14%, reasoning–action mismatch 13.98% — so the figures moved between revisions and both sets circulate; cite one version consistently, preferably v3 (sourced: v3 PDF Figure 2 vs. v2 HTML of the same arXiv record).

The measurement itself is credible by annotation-study standards: 1,600+ annotated traces, inter-annotator Cohen's kappa 0.88 (0.84 in the final round), and an LLM-as-judge pipeline (OpenAI o1, few-shot) that scores 94% accuracy, kappa 0.77, F1 0.80 against human annotation, with kappa 0.79 generalizing to systems unseen during development (OpenManus, Magentic-One on MMLU/GAIA). Human annotation cost 20+ hours per annotator (all sourced: arXiv:2503.13657, Sections 3–4).

Two more calibration points from MAST before reaching for fixes: interventions help but do not rescue. Adding a verification step to ChatDev bought +15.6 percentage points absolute on ProgramDev; improved role and prompt specification bought +9.4 points — and even with both, ChatDev remained far below reliable, from a 33.3% correctness baseline (sourced: arXiv:2503.13657, Section 5 / Appendix H).

Benchmark pass rates also overstate reliability in a specific, measured way: they are usually single-run numbers, and reliability decays sharply under repetition even for single agents. On τ-bench (Yao et al., arXiv:2406.12045, ICLR 2025), a GPT-4o function-calling agent scores ~61% pass^1 on τ-retail and ~35% on τ-airline, but pass^8 — all 8 independent runs succeed — collapses to ~25% on retail, a ~60% relative drop; τ-bench introduced the pass^k metric precisely because headline pass^1 overstates production-grade reliability (sourced: the paper plus Sierra's engineering blog). Derived consequence: any multi-agent failure rate quoted from a single-run benchmark is a *floor* on what repeated production use would experience, since production means running the same class of task many times and each independent run is another chance to fail.

For a baseline on realistic long-horizon work, TheAgentCompany (Xu et al., CMU, arXiv:2412.14161, NeurIPS 2025 Datasets & Benchmarks) put agents in a simulated software company — 175 long-horizon professional tasks against Gitlab, RocketChat, OwnCloud. The best agents fully complete only ~24–30% of tasks: Claude 3.5 Sonnet 24%, Claude 3.7 Sonnet 26.3% full / 36.4% with partial credit, Gemini 2.5 Pro 30.3% — roughly 70–76% failure on end-to-end knowledge work *before* any multi-agent coordination overhead (sourced: arXiv:2412.14161; figures cross-checked against the NeurIPS listing and multiple writeups rather than the paper PDF itself).

The nearest things to production evidence are two datapoints, each disqualified as a task-level failure rate for a different reason. Anthropic reports its multi-agent research system (Opus 4 lead + Sonnet 4 subagents) beat single-agent Opus 4 by 90.2% on an internal research eval, at a measured cost of ~15x the tokens of a chat interaction (single agents ~4x); on BrowseComp, three factors explained 95% of performance variance, token spend alone 80% (sourced: Anthropic engineering blog, "How we built our multi-agent research system", 2025 — but note this is an internal, unaudited eval, not production telemetry). Datadog's State of AI Engineering is genuinely aggregated production data across thousands of customer companies, but at the wrong level: in Feb 2026, 5% of all LLM call spans errored (60% of those rate-limit errors); by Mar 2026, 2% errored (~8.4M rate-limit errors, roughly a third of failures); agentic-framework adoption roughly doubled year over year (~9% → ~18% of orgs), and 59% of agentic requests hit a single service while only 18% chain 3+ service calls (sourced: datadoghq.com/state-of-ai-engineering). These are span-level infrastructure error rates — an agent can complete every API call successfully and still fail its task, so a 2% span error rate says nothing about task-level failure and must not be quoted as one (derived: task failure and call failure are different denominators).

Whether multi-agent orchestration even beats a single agent is genuinely mixed and task-dependent, with measured reversals in both directions — but this evidence is **uncertain**: a clinical-workload preprint reports orchestrated multi-agent accuracy degrading 90.6%→65.3% as batch size grows from 5 to 80 tasks while a single agent collapses 73.1%→16.6% (multi-agent wins under load), while on GISclaw geospatial tasks single agents beat dual-agent setups by 3–10 points across most cloud models, with Qwen2.5-Coder-14B the lone exception (52.0%→78.7% under orchestration) (uncertain: medRxiv 2025.08.22.25334049 is a non-peer-reviewed preprint whose PDF could not be fetched, GISclaw is arXiv:2603.26845, and both sets of numbers came via search-result summaries with sample sizes unverified — treat as "a preprint reports", not established).

Two things you cannot do with these numbers, both consequences of the production gap. First, when a failure does occur, attributing it is itself measurably unsolved: on the Who&When dataset (127 hand-annotated failure logs from LLM multi-agent systems), the best automated method identifies the responsible agent only 53.5% of the time and pinpoints the decisive error step only 14.2% of the time; SOTA reasoning models (o1, DeepSeek-R1) do not reach practical usability (sourced: Zhang et al., "Which Agent Causes Task Failures and When?", arXiv:2505.00212, ICML 2025). Second, the widely circulated "70–95% of AI agents fail in production" claim (vendor blogs, e.g. Fiddler AI's "AI Agent Failure Rate") is not measured runtime telemetry — derived, by tracing it to its inputs: it conflates MAST's 41–86.7% *benchmark* failure rates with the pseudo-statistic's second input, a figure commonly attributed to MIT's "GenAI Divide" survey (95% of GenAI pilots yielding zero measurable return — not independently verified in this research, and in any case a pilot-ROI number, not a runtime failure rate) — into a composite no one measured. A related SSRN preprint (abstract 7041478) claiming 75.17% of production multi-agent failures are silent "gray failures" could not be verified — the fetch returned 403, the authors are unestablished, and no deployments are named — so that figure must not be cited as fact (uncertain).

The honest summary: on benchmarks, state-of-the-art multi-agent systems fail 41–87% of their tasks, single-run; repetition makes it worse, interventions buy 9–16 points, attribution barely works, and the production number everyone wants has never been published. If someone quotes you a production multi-agent failure rate, ask which live deployment it was measured on — as of July 2026, there is no public answer to that question.
