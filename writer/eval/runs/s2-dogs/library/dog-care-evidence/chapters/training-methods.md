"Be the alpha" and correction-based training are not neutral folk wisdom — they run against explicit positions of the veterinary behavior profession, which favors reward-based methods.

**The core position.** The American Veterinary Society of Animal Behavior (AVSAB) *Humane Dog Training* position statement (2021) states there is no evidence that aversive training methods are more effective than reward-based methods in any context (sourced, AVSAB 2021). The statement names tools it advises against — electronic/shock collars, prong collars, choke chains, and leash corrections (sourced). Its evidence base is a review of 21 studies (sourced). It enumerates harms associated with punishment-based training: inhibited learning, increased fear and aggression, and risk of injury to both animals and handlers (sourced).

**The anti-"alpha" stance is a named-body position, not folklore.** AVSAB maintains separate position statements specifically on Dominance Theory and on the Use of Punishment (sourced, AVSAB). This matters for how the claim should be argued: rejecting the pack-hierarchy/"alpha" model is the documented position of a professional veterinary-behavior body, so it can be cited as such rather than asserted as personal preference (derived — the significance of the claim follows from its being a named-body statement; that the statements exist is sourced).

**Bottom line.** When someone frames dominance/correction training as simply "how dogs work," the evidence-based counter is specific and citable: AVSAB finds no efficacy advantage for aversive methods and documents their harms, and separately rejects dominance theory as a training framework (derived from the AVSAB statements above).
