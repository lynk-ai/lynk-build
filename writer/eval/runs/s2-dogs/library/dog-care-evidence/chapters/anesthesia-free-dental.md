Anesthesia-free dental cleaning treats what is visible and leaves the actual disease untouched — which is why the veterinary dental specialty opposes it.

**The position.** The American Veterinary Dental College (AVDC) has held a position statement since 2004 against dental scaling performed without anesthesia, which it calls "nonprofessional dental scaling" (sourced, AVDC position statement). The stated reason is diagnostic and mechanical: a conscious animal cannot be properly examined and cannot be scaled subgingivally — below the gumline — which is exactly where periodontal disease occurs (sourced, AVDC).

**The prevalence contradiction shows what conscious exams miss.** Periodontal disease is diagnosed in roughly 9.3–18.2% of dogs on conscious visual examination, but in 44–100% of dogs when examined under anesthesia (sourced, Wallis & Holcombe, *Journal of Small Animal Practice*, 2020; O'Neill VetCompass 2021). The gap between those ranges is the point: the disease is largely subgingival, so an awake visual exam — the only exam anesthesia-free cleaning allows — systematically under-detects it (derived from the two prevalence ranges above).

**Bottom line.** Anesthesia-free dental cleaning can make teeth look cleaner while leaving subgingival periodontal disease both undiagnosed and untreated; the anesthesia is what enables the diagnosis and the treatment, not just the cleaning (derived from the AVDC rationale and the prevalence gap above).
