Among dog-care interventions, keeping a dog lean has unusually strong evidence: a controlled lifelong study, not correlational data, shows it extends both lifespan and healthspan.

**The study.** Kealy et al., *JAVMA*, 2002 — the Purina Life Span Study — followed 48 Labrador Retrievers over 14 years. Half were fed 25% less than their pair-matched littermates to maintain a lean body condition throughout life (sourced, Kealy et al. 2002). Median lifespan was 13.0 years in the lean-fed group versus 11.2 years in the control group — roughly a 1.8-year, about 15%, extension attributable to body condition alone (sourced, same study). Because the two groups were pair-matched littermates differing only in food quantity, this is a controlled lifelong cohort rather than a correlation (derived from the study design described above).

**It extended healthspan, not just lifespan.** The lean-fed dogs showed delayed onset of chronic disease, later visible signs of aging, later radiographic evidence of osteoarthritis, and healthier metabolic markers (sourced, Kealy et al. 2002). So the benefit is not merely more years but more healthy years (derived from those endpoints).

**Bottom line.** The single best-supported "diet" lever for canine longevity in this book is not a food type or supplement — it is lifelong lean body condition achieved through controlled portions (derived from the strength and design of the Kealy study relative to the weaker or contested diet claims elsewhere in this book, e.g. [grain-free-and-dcm](grain-free-and-dcm.md)).
