Flat-faced conformation is not a cosmetic preference; it is a documented cause of airway disease and shortened lifespan, and it is now the subject of breeding law and functional screening in several countries.

**The mechanism.** Selective breeding shrank the skull without proportionally shrinking the soft tissue inside it, so the excess soft tissue obstructs the airway — brachycephalic obstructive airway syndrome (BOAS). Risk rises sharply and non-linearly as the relative muzzle length shortens; craniofacial ratio predicts BOAS risk but has been cautioned against as a sole predictor (sourced, Packer et al. *PLOS One* 2015; Liu et al. 2017).

**Regulation: the Netherlands.** The Dutch Animals Act dates to 2014; in 2019 the Ministry set a breeding criterion that dogs with a muzzle shorter than one-third of skull length may not be bred (sourced). In 2020 the Dutch Kennel Club (Raad van Beheer) restricted the registration of 12 breeds and promoted outcrossing (sourced, Raad van Beheer 2020).

**Screening: the UK functional approach.** The Respiratory Function Grading Scheme, developed by the Kennel Club with the University of Cambridge (2019; Ladlow et al., *In Practice*, 2021), grades dogs by a functional exercise test before breeding — an evidence-based alternative to grading conformation by measurement alone (sourced, Ladlow et al. 2021). The two approaches differ in what they measure: the Dutch muzzle-ratio rule is anatomical, the UK scheme is functional (derived from the two schemes' descriptions above).

**The lifespan cost.** VetCompass life tables (O'Neill et al., *Scientific Reports* 12, 2022) reported a French Bulldog life expectancy of 4.53 years versus 11.23 years for dogs overall; English Bulldog 7.4 years and Pug 7.7 years (sourced, O'Neill et al. 2022). The four shortest-lived breeds in that analysis were all brachycephalic (sourced, same study) — which is why "they're just built that way" understates a measurable survival penalty (derived from the life-table figures).
