The "annual booster for everything" recall is out of step with current guidance. For adult core vaccines the endorsed interval is three years, and antibody titer testing is a recognized option.

**Adult core vaccines: every three years, not annually.** The WSAVA Vaccination Guidelines (Squires et al., *Journal of Small Animal Practice*, 2024) state that adult core vaccines — canine distemper virus (CDV), canine adenovirus (CAV), and canine parvovirus (CPV) — should be re-administered no more often than every three years, not annually (sourced, WSAVA 2024). The rationale is documented duration of immunity: modern modified-live-virus (MLV) core vaccines confer immunity lasting many years (sourced, WSAVA 2024). This is the substantive reason the interval changed — it is not a cost or convenience compromise (derived from the duration-of-immunity rationale stated in the guidelines).

**Titer testing is a recognized alternative.** WSAVA has partly endorsed antibody titer testing since 2016, reaffirmed in 2024 (sourced, WSAVA). Lab-based titer testing is preferred over in-house test kits (sourced). A positive CDV/CPV/CAV titer measured at least 4 weeks after the final puppy dose indicates the dog does not need re-vaccination for three years (sourced, WSAVA).

**The puppy series and the 6-month refinement.** The puppy series is given every 2–4 weeks, with the final dose at 16 weeks of age or older (sourced, WSAVA). The 2024 guidance retains a revaccination at 26 weeks (6 months) for dogs that have not been titer-tested — a refinement of the older 12-month booster that addresses the problem of maternally derived antibodies blocking the puppy-series response (sourced, WSAVA 2024).

**Rabies is the exception.** Rabies vaccination stays on its legally mandated schedule regardless of the above — that schedule is set by law/jurisdiction, not by immunity duration alone (sourced, WSAVA / legal requirement).

**Bottom line.** An annual recall notice for core vaccines is not consistent with current WSAVA guidance; a triennial interval, or titer-guided timing, is the evidence-based default for adult dogs, with rabies handled separately on its legal schedule (derived from the WSAVA positions above).
