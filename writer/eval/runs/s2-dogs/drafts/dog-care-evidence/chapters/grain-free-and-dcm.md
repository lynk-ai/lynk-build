The "grain-free food causes heart disease" claim is real but weaker and more contested than its popular framing. This chapter separates what the FDA investigation established from what it explicitly declined to conclude.

**What the FDA reported.** The FDA Center for Veterinary Medicine (CVM) opened a public investigation into a possible link between certain diets and canine dilated cardiomyopathy in July 2018 (sourced, FDA CVM). In its June 2019 update the FDA reported that more than 90% of the implicated diets were labeled grain-free and about 93% contained peas and/or lentils (sourced, FDA CVM June 2019 update).

**Taurine deficiency is not the confirmed mechanism.** A natural early hypothesis was that these diets caused taurine deficiency (a known cause of DCM in some species and breeds). The FDA's own testing complicated this: grain-free foods had, on average, the same taurine, protein, and fat content as grain-containing foods (sourced, FDA CVM). So taurine deficiency is *not* the established mechanism, and the mechanism connecting these diets to DCM remains unidentified (sourced, FDA CVM).

**The FDA has stepped back from a causal claim.** In December 2022 the FDA stated it did not have enough data to establish a causal relationship between these diets and DCM, and announced it would not provide further updates until there was meaningful new research to report (sourced, FDA CVM statement Dec 2022, as reported in the AKC 2023 update). This is a material walk-back from how the 2018–2019 coverage was widely read.

**Diet-associated DCM appears partly reversible — unlike genetic DCM.** Freeman et al., published in *JAVMA* 253(11), 1 December 2018, described a case series in which 24 Golden Retrievers with confirmed DCM and low whole-blood taurine improved echocardiographically after a diet change plus taurine supplementation over roughly 12–24 months — all but one of the dogs improved (sourced, Freeman et al. JAVMA 2018). A separate group of 7 non-Golden dogs in the same body of work also improved (sourced, Freeman et al. JAVMA 2018). This partial reversibility is the clinically important contrast: hereditary/genetic DCM (seen classically in breeds like Doberman Pinschers) is not diet-correctable in this way (derived — the reversibility-on-diet-change finding is what distinguishes the diet-associated form from the genetic form the reviewer families are known for; the diet finding is sourced, the contrast is the reasoning).

**A contested counter-signal.** Reported grain-free pet-food sales rose roughly fivefold between 2011 and 2019 without a corresponding population-level rise in DCM incidence, and a review in the *Journal of Animal Science* 98(6), skaa155 (2020) framed the disease as multifactorial rather than diet-caused (sourced, but flagged contested — this is a counter-argument in an active scientific debate, not a settled finding, and the absence of a documented incidence rise is itself an argument-from-absence that surveillance gaps could explain).

**Figures to treat with caution.** Widely circulated numbers — a cumulative "1,382-case FDA tally" and "16 brands with ≥10 cases each" — come from secondary summaries that were not re-verified for this chapter (uncertain — do not present these as sourced FDA figures without checking the primary FDA update they derive from).

**Bottom line for a decision.** The evidence supports treating BEG/grain-free diets as a *plausible contributing factor* in some DCM cases and supports veterinary workup (including diet history and echocardiography) for an affected dog, but it does not support a blanket claim that grain-free food causes heart disease in dogs generally (derived from the FDA's own no-causality position plus the contested incidence data above).
