The "raw is natural, therefore safe" framing is not supported by surveillance data: raw diets carry documented pathogen contamination and a real human-household (zoonotic) risk.

**The FDA sampling data.** In an FDA CVM study running October 2010 to July 2012, 196 samples of commercial raw pet food were tested: 15 were positive for *Salmonella* and 32 for *Listeria monocytogenes*, and raw pet food was the most-contaminated pet-food category in the study (sourced, FDA CVM 2010–2012 study).

**The professional-body position.** The AVMA adopted a policy in 2012 discouraging the feeding of animal-source protein that has not been processed to eliminate pathogens (sourced, AVMA policy 2012). The human-health rationale is that healthy dogs can shed pathogens subclinically — appearing well while excreting organisms — which endangers young, elderly, pregnant, and immunocompromised members of the household (sourced, AVMA).

**The pathogen list is broader than Salmonella.** Named organisms associated with raw animal-source diets extend to *Campylobacter*, *Clostridium*, *E. coli*, *Listeria*, highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) virus, *Mycobacterium bovis*, and enterotoxigenic *Staphylococcus aureus* (sourced, CVMA position statement; AVMA).

**Bottom line.** The evidence supports treating raw diets as a documented food-safety hazard to both dog and household rather than a neutral lifestyle choice — the contamination is measured and the zoonotic pathway is the stated reason multiple veterinary bodies discourage it (derived from the sampling data and the association policies above).
