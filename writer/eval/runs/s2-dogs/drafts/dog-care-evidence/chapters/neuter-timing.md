The default advice "neuter every dog at about six months" is not supported by the strongest recent evidence, which shows the risks of early neutering are breed- and size-specific rather than uniform.

**The Golden Retriever signal is large.** Hart et al., *Frontiers in Veterinary Science* 7:388, published 7 July 2020, examined 35 breeds. In Golden Retrievers, dogs neutered before 12 months had roughly 2–4x the risk of joint disorders, with the risk worst when neutered before 6 months (sourced, Hart et al. Frontiers 7:388). Female Golden Retrievers showed 2–4x the cancer risk of intact females at essentially any neuter age (sourced, same paper). Crucially, the same study found that for many other breeds the age of neutering made no measurable difference to these risks (sourced) — which is exactly why a single across-the-board age is the wrong instrument.

**In mixed breeds, joint risk tracks body size.** The companion study, Hart et al., *Frontiers in Veterinary Science* 7:472 (2020), on mixed-breed dogs found joint-disorder risk is body-size-driven: dogs of 20 kg or more neutered before 1 year had about 3x the joint-disorder risk, while dogs under 20 kg showed no increased joint-disorder risk from early neutering (sourced, Hart et al. Frontiers 7:472). For female mixed-breed dogs above roughly 43 lb, joint-disorder risk rose from about 4% in intact dogs to 10–12% when spayed before one year (sourced, same paper).

**Cancer and joint effects are decoupled, and both are breed-specific.** In the mixed-breed study there was no significant neuter-associated cancer increase in any weight category (sourced, Frontiers 7:472). Among small breeds in the purebred study, only the Boston Terrier and Shih Tzu showed a significant neuter-associated cancer increase (sourced, Frontiers 7:388). So the joint story and the cancer story do not move together, and neither generalizes across breeds — the practical upshot is that neuter timing should be a per-breed (and for mixed breeds, per-size) conversation, not a calendar default (derived from the two studies' breed/size-specific findings).

**A figure to attribute carefully.** The often-cited "about 7% urinary incontinence in spayed female German Shepherds" figure does not come from the 2020 Hart studies; it traces to an earlier (2016) German Shepherd–specific paper (uncertain here — do not attribute this figure to the 2020 studies; cite it only to that earlier GSD-specific paper if used).
